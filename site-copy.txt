HERO (intro screen, before first question)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------
Headline:   Build the stack for where you are,
            not where you might be.

Body:       Tell it your stage, your team, and the one thing actually slowing
            you down. It recommends a lean stack built around that problem,
            sized to your budget, with the reasoning for every pick and where
            the free option is good enough.

Button:     Build my stack →

Footer note: Shaped by working alongside founders at two early stage startups,
            where the right lightweight system usually beat the expensive one
            nobody kept up.


QUESTIONS (5 total)
-----------------------------------------------------------------------------

Q1: Stage
Prompt:  Where is the company right now?
Help:    This decides how much process you actually need. Too much too early
         is its own drag.
Options:
  - Still figuring it out — Pre-product, validating the idea
  - Building the first version — MVP in progress, pre-launch
  - Launched with early users — Some traction, maybe some revenue
  - Growing fast - Revenue climbing, starting to hire

Q2: Team
Prompt:  Who is actually doing the work?
Help:    Team size decides whether you need coordination tools or just a
         shared doc and good habits.
Options:
  - Just me — Solo or a co-founder
  - A tight team — 3 to 8 people
  - A growing team — 9 to 20 people
  - Getting sizeable — 20+ people

Q3: The real problem
Prompt:  What is genuinely keeping you up at night?
Help:    Pick the one thing that, if it were fixed tomorrow, would unblock
         the most. The stack is built around this.
Options:
  - I can't find enough customers — Growth and go to market
  - I'm not sure we're building the right thing — Product direction and feedback
  - I'm drowning in busywork — Admin and manual tasks eat the day
  - Things keep falling through the cracks — No system, stuff gets forgotten
  - We can't keep up with customers — Support and inbound messages
  - We look amateur next to competitors — Brand, design, and positioning
  - Runway and fundraising — Cash, investors, finance
  - Hiring is chaos — Finding and onboarding people

Q4 — Budget
Prompt:  What can you spend on tools each month?
Help:    Early on, free tiers and startup credits cover far more than
         founders expect. This filters out overkill.
Options:
  - Basically nothing — Free tiers and credits only
  - A little — Under £150 a month
  - We have some budget — £150 to £800 a month

Q5 — Ways of working
Prompt:  How does the team like to work?
Help:    A stack the team resents is a stack nobody updates. This tunes the
         picks for fit.
Options:
  - Async and written — Docs over meetings
  - Fast and scrappy — Move first, tidy later
  - Structured — Clear process and owners

--- FOUNDATION ---

Notion: Company wiki, docs, light databases | Free tier · paid from ~£10/user
  Reason (if team = solo): One home for everything before you have the
    headcount to specialise. Free plan has no page limit for solo use.
  Reason (otherwise): A single source of truth so context stops living only
    in people's heads. Ends the same question being asked five times.
  Why this: Over a pile of Google Docs, because databases and templates turn
    notes into reusable systems.
  Tip: Notion for Startups: up to 6 months of Business + AI free for eligible new non-paying startups.

Google Workspace: Email, docs, drive, calendar | Free on Gmail · Business from ~£5/user
  Reason: The boring backbone everything else plugs into. Custom domain
    email also makes early outreach look legitimate.
  Tip: Free on an @gmail address. Add a domain only when you start selling.

Coda: Docs that behave like apps | Free tier · paid from ~£9/user
  Reason: When your docs need buttons, logic, and live tables, Coda goes
    further than Notion. Good for structured teams building internal tools.
  Why this: Over Notion specifically when you want interactive docs, not
    just pretty ones.

1Password: Shared password and secrets manager | From ~£3.70/user
  Reason: The moment two people share a login, passwords in a spreadsheet
    become a security incident waiting to happen. Cheap insurance.


--- COMMUNICATION ---

Slack: Team messaging and integrations | Free tier is generous
  Reason (if async style): Channels keep decisions searchable instead of
    buried in DMs. Set it quiet, keep the record.
  Reason (otherwise): Fast coordination without another meeting. Free tier is generous, but it starts to strain once teams grow past ~20 people.
  Why this: Over WhatsApp for work, because search and channels mean
    context does not vanish up the scroll.

Loom: Screen recordings instead of meetings | Free tier · paid from ~£13/mo
  Reason: A two minute recording replaces a thirty minute call for anything
    you can show rather than discuss. Async teams live on this.

Google Meet: Video calls | Free with Workspace
  Reason: Free with Workspace and fine for most calls. No 40 minute cap to
    work around, and nothing extra to buy early on.
  Why this: Over Zoom early, because you are already paying for Workspace
    and Zoom's free-tier time limit will annoy you.

Discord: Community and user channels | Free
  Reason: If your early users cluster in a community, Discord is where they
    already are. Free, and great for building in public.


--- GROWTH ---

HubSpot free CRM: Track leads, deals, conversations | Free for 2 users · paid from ~£18/user
  Reason: Once you have more than a handful of prospects, memory stops
    scaling and deals go cold in the gaps. Free tier covers early stage
    fully.
  Why this: Over a spreadsheet, because it reminds you to follow up. A
    sheet never nudges you.

Attio: Modern, flexible CRM | Free for 3 users · paid from ~£29/user
  Reason: A CRM you shape around how you actually sell, rather than bending
    your process to fit. Founder favourite, and the free tier fits a small
    team.
  Why this: Over HubSpot when you want a data model that matches your
    business, not a rigid contacts-and-deals template.

Apollo.io: Find prospects and run outreach | Free tier · paid from ~£35/mo
  Reason: Build targeted lists and sequence outreach without enterprise
    sales-tool prices. Right-sized for founder-led sales.

Instantly: Cold email sequences | From ~£35/mo 
  Reason: When outbound becomes a real channel, Instantly keeps sequencing
    simple with flat pricing that suits a lean team. Lemlist is the
    pricier, more polished option once outbound is a dedicated function.
  Why this: Over Lemlist for an early team, because flat pricing beats
    per-seat when you are small and watching spend.

Plausible or GA4: Website and product analytics | GA4 free · Plausible from ~£9/mo
  Reason: You cannot improve what you cannot see. GA4 if free is the
    priority, Plausible if you want simple and privacy-friendly.

PostHog: Product analytics and session insight | Generous free tier
  Reason: Shows what users actually do inside the product, not just how
    many arrived. The free tier is large enough to run on for a long time.
  Why this: Over GA4 for product depth, because GA4 tells you about
    traffic, PostHog tells you about behaviour.

Buffer: Schedule social, build in public | Free tier · paid from ~£4/channel/mo
  Reason: Early distribution is often the founder posting consistently.
    Scheduling removes the daily friction so it actually happens. Note it
    is priced per channel, so costs rise with each account.


--- DESIGN ---

Figma: UI, product, and marketing design | Free tier · full seat from ~£18/mo
  Reason (if problem = brand): The industry standard for anything visual.
    Even non-designers can tidy up a deck or mockup here. Free tier covers
    early needs, the full editor seat is what you pay for once designing
    properly.
  Reason (otherwise): One place for product mockups, quick graphics, and
    shared design files. Free tier is enough until you have a dedicated
    designer.

Canva: Fast graphics without a designer | Free tier · Pro from ~£13/mo
  Reason: Social posts, one-pagers, and decks that look professional in
    minutes. This is what keeps a founder-run brand from looking homemade.
  Why this: Over Figma for quick marketing assets, because templates get
    you to done faster when you are not a designer.
  Tip: Canva for Startups gives eligible early teams Pro features free.

Framer or Webflow: Marketing site without a developer | Free tier · paid from ~£10/mo
  Reason: A credible landing page you can edit yourself, no engineer
    needed. Framer is faster to learn, Webflow gives more control.
  Why this: Over a custom-coded site early, because your time is better
    spent talking to users than maintaining a codebase.

Pitch: Collaborative pitch and sales decks | Free tier · paid from ~£16/mo
  Reason: Founders live in decks: investor, sales, hiring. Pitch makes them
    look designed without the PowerPoint fight, and the team can work in
    one live at once.
  Why this: Over PowerPoint or raw Google Slides, because the templates and
    live collaboration save real hours on the decks you rebuild constantly.

Brandmark or Looka: Starter logo and brand basics | One-off from ~£14
  Reason: A clean starter identity for near nothing, so you look
    intentional before you can afford a designer. Replace it later when
    brand matters more.

Unsplash + Coolors: Free imagery and colour palettes | Free
  Reason: Good free photography and a palette generator cover most early
    visual needs. Small touches that make a scrappy brand feel deliberate.


--- PRODUCT ---

Linear: Issue tracking and product planning | Free tier · paid from ~£8/user
  Reason (if problem = building): Direction comes from a clear, reorderable
    queue, not more meetings. Fast enough that engineers actually keep it
    current.
  Reason (otherwise): Keeps the roadmap visible so priorities stop
    drifting. Light enough not to slow a small team.
  Why this: Over Jira at this stage, because Jira's power is overhead you
    do not need until you are much bigger.

GitHub: Code hosting and version control | Free for small teams
  Reason: Where your code lives, reviews happen, and history is safe.
    Non-negotiable the moment more than one person writes code.

Vercel or Netlify: Deploy the product and sites | Free tier · paid from ~£14/mo
  Reason: Push code and it is live, with previews for every change.
    Removes a whole category of deployment headache for small teams.

Tagged feedback database: Capture and theme user feedback | Free in Notion · Dovetail paid.
  Reason (if budget = zero): A tagged Notion database does most of this for
    free. Structure feedback so patterns surface before you spend on a
    dedicated tool.
  Reason (otherwise): Turns scattered comments into themes you can act on.
    Feedback you cannot search is feedback you quietly ignore.

Canny: Public feedback and roadmap board | Free tier · paid from ~£70/mo
  Reason: Lets users post and vote on requests in the open, so you
    prioritise with evidence instead of guesses and gut feel.

Cal.com or Calendly: Book user interviews easily | Free tier works
  Reason: Talking to users is the highest-leverage early activity. Remove
    every gram of scheduling friction so more conversations happen.


--- OPERATIONS ---

Zapier or Make: Automate handoffs between tools | Free tier · paid from ~£7/mo (Make) or ~£25/mo (Zapier)
  Reason: Every manual copy-paste between two tools is a task that will
    eventually be forgotten. Automate the handoffs first, the clever stuff
    never.
  Why this: Make is cheaper and more flexible for complex flows. Zapier is
    simpler if you just want it working today.

Templated processes with owners: Repeatable workflows, one name per step | Free in Notion
  Reason: Things fall through cracks because steps have no owner, not
    because you lack a tool. A name against each step fixes most of it.

Tally: Forms, surveys, and intake | Free (generous) · Pro from ~£20/mo
  Reason (if budget = zero): Beautiful forms with a genuinely generous free
    tier, edited like a Notion doc. The zero-budget answer for lead
    capture, surveys, and applications.
  Reason (otherwise): Fast, clean forms for lead capture, surveys, and
    applications, feeding straight into the rest of your stack.
  Why this: Over Typeform when budget is tight, because Tally's free tier
    is far more generous for the same job.

AI assistant workflow: Draft, summarise, clear repetitive admin | Free to start
  Reason: Most founder busywork is drafting, summarising, and reformatting.
    A few saved AI prompts for the repeat jobs claw back hours a week for
    near nothing.

Airtable: Relational database for ops | Free tier · paid from ~£14/user
  Reason: When Notion databases start creaking under real operational
    volume, Airtable's relational structure and automations hold up
    better.
  Why this: Over a Notion database when the data is genuinely operational:
    inventory, pipelines, anything with thousands of rows.

Miro or FigJam: Workshops and strategy mapping | Free tier · paid from ~£5/user
  Reason: When you need to map a process, run a planning session, or think
    visually as a team, a shared canvas beats a doc. FigJam is free if you
    are already in Figma.
  Why this: FigJam over Miro if you already use Figma, since it is
    included. Miro if you want the deeper workshop features.

Simple OKR or goals doc: Keep everyone pointed the same way | Free
  Reason: As the team grows, a one-page set of quarterly goals with owners
    stops good people rowing in different directions. Start as a doc, not
    software.


--- CUSTOMER SUPPORT ---

Front: Shared inbox for support@ and hello@ | Free trial · paid from ~£25/seat
  Reason: Early support is a shared email address descending into chaos:
    replies missed, two people answering the same thing. A shared inbox
    gives every message an owner without a heavy help desk.
  Why this: Over forwarding support@ to a personal inbox, because that
    quietly drops customers and nobody knows who replied.

Plain: Lightweight, fast customer support | No free tier · paid from ~£25/seat
  Reason: Support tooling that feels like Linear: fast, clean, and built for product-led teams rather than call centres. A good first support tool for a technical early team.
  Why this: Over Intercom or Zendesk early, because it is lighter to run and better aligned with B2B SaaS workflows.


--- FINANCE ---

Runway model in Sheets: Cash, runway, scenario planning | Free
  Reason: Before any finance software, a clean sheet showing runway in
    months is what you and investors both need. Start here.
  Why this: Over finance software on day one, because a founder who knows
    their runway cold beats one with a pretty dashboard.

Xero: Bookkeeping and invoicing | From ~£7/mo (Simple) or ~£16/mo (Ignite)
  Reason: Clean books now save a painful cleanup at your first raise or
    year end. Non-negotiable once real money is moving. Stronger in the UK
    than QuickBooks, which is the main alternative.
  Why this: Over QuickBooks for a UK company, because Xero's interface and
    local fit are cleaner. QuickBooks is the alternative if you prefer it.

Stripe: Take payments online | ~1.5% + 20p per UK card
  Reason: The default way to charge customers, with the developer support
    to match. If you are collecting revenue, this is the safe starting
    point.

Pleo: Company cards and spend control | Free trial · Starter from ~£9.50/mo · Essential £45/mo
  Reason: Once more than one person spends money, per-person cards with
    built-in limits and receipt capture beat chasing expense claims. Pleo
    is UK and EU native. Ramp is the US alternative.
  Why this: Over Ramp or Brex for a UK team, because Pleo is built for UK
    and EU, while Ramp and Brex are US-first.

Cap table in Sheets or Carta: Track equity and ownership | Free (Sheets) · Carta paid
  Reason: Know exactly who owns what before you raise. A clean sheet is
    fine early, move to Carta when the cap table gets complex.

AI investor research workflow: Source, qualify, and track investors | Free to build
  Reason: Fundraising prep is mostly structured research: who fits, how to
    reach them, what to say. An automated sourcing and qualification
    workflow cuts the manual effort sharply and keeps outreach organised.
  Tip: This is the layer I built hands-on: automated investor sourcing,
    qualification, structured lead tracking, and tailored outreach to
    improve fundraising readiness.


--- PEOPLE ---

Simple candidate pipeline: Track applicants by stage and owner | Free in Notion · Ashby when funded
  Reason (if team = solo OR budget = zero): A pipeline board beats a
    spreadsheet and costs nothing. Every candidate has a stage and an
    owner, so nobody goes cold.
  Reason (otherwise): Hiring falls apart in the handoffs. A real pipeline
    keeps candidates warm and the process consistent.
  Why this: Over an inbox and a spreadsheet, because a pipeline shows at a
    glance who is stuck and where.

Ashby: Applicant tracking for serious hiring | Paid, for funded teams
  Reason: When hiring becomes a constant, a proper ATS with analytics beats
    a manual board. Worth it once you are hiring several roles at once.

Tally or Typeform: Application and intake forms | Tally free · Typeform from ~£30/mo
  Reason: A clean application form that people actually finish, feeding
    straight into your pipeline. Tally covers it free, Typeform is the
    paid option if you want its polish.

Deel: Payroll and contractor management | Contractors from ~£35 each/mo; EOR from ~£445/employee/mo
  Reason: Paying people correctly and on time is not where you want to
    improvise. Deel is strong for UK and international contractors, and
    has grown into a fuller HR platform with a Core HRIS from ~£4/employee
    and hiring tools layered on, so it scales as your team does. Gusto is
    the US-focused alternative.
  Why this: Over Gusto for a UK or international team, because Gusto is
    US-focused while Deel handles cross-border hiring cleanly.

Onboarding checklist template: Repeatable, productive first week | Free
  Reason: The cheapest retention tool there is. A new hire useful in week
    one stays. Write it once, reuse it every time.
